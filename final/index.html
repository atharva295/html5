Life Cycle of AscarisLumbricoides

Anterior view of the hip and pelvis, showing attachment of ligaments to the femur, ilium, ischium, and pubis.

Introduction

AscarisLumbricoides belongs to phylum Nematoda. The infection caused by it is known as Ascariasis which is most common helminthic infection in world. It is longest Nematode in human intestine.
  • Ascaris can grow up to 35 cm.
  • These worms are also known as “round worms”.
  • Mostly found in tropical area and usually infect children.

Life cycle:

  • Infective form : eggs containing rhabditiformlarvae
  • Definitive host : Humans
  • Intermediate host: absent (no intermediate host)
  • Mode of infection: ingestion of eggs
  • Habitat: small intestine

Explanation of the life cycle:

  1. Eggs are ingested by the human trough contaminated food or any other sources which then go to intestine.
  2. In intestine rhabditiform larvae converts into filariform larvae.
  3. Filariform larva enters into circulation by piercing the gastrointestinal mucosa.
  4. Then this larva travel through the blood and comes into right ventricle of heart. From their it goes to the lung along with blood flow.
  5. In lungs the larvae enters into alveoli by piercing the alveolar wall.
  6. Then it ascends upwards in trachea and from there it re-enters into esophagus and goes to stomach and intestine.
  7. In intestine larvae mature into male and female worms. These undergo reproduction and forms the new eggs.
  8. These worms majorly grows in jejunum of small intestine and female worm can produce around 200,000 eggs per day.
  9. These eggs are then passed through the faeces and the cycle continues.

Note: Newly formed eggs require 3 weeks to embryonateand togets infectious.

Pathogenesis:

AscarisLumbricoides causes following manifestation in humans. • Loffler's syndrome:
  1. It is a type of pulmonary eosinophilic syndrome
  2. When the larvae pierce the alveolar wall this provokes severe type 1 hypersensitivity reaction.
  3. This results in severe pneumonitis
  4. Clinical features: cough, dyspnoea, and blood streaks in sputum can be seen
• At the level of intestine
  1. Malnutrition.
  2. Bowel obstruction due to large number of worms in intestine.
  3. Intussusception
  4. Several cases can lead to perforation of intestine.
• Other manifestation: These occurs due to entering larvae at that site or respected duct –
  1. Appendicitis.
  2. Cholecystitis
  3. Pancreatitis
  4. Airway obstruction

Investigation:

• Stool examination Fertilised and unfertilised Ascaris eggs can be seen in faeces.
  1. Fertilised egg: these are of around 50-70 micron size and they can be bile stained. They contain unsegmented ovum.
  2. Unfertilised eggs: these are of around 90 micron sizeand they can’t be bile stained. They contain atrophied ovum.
• X- ray chest:
In case of Loffler’s syndrome- patchy rounded infiltrates are seen.

Treatment:

Drug of choice : Albendazole 400 mg × in single dose
Other alternatives:
  1. Mebendazole × 3 days
  2. Ivermectincan be used.
  3. Pyrantelpamoate× single dose

Precautions:

  1. Sanitisation of children play areas.
  2. Avoiding use of human faeces as fertiliser.
  3. Hospitalisation of the inflected patient.
